2. Cognitive Dissonance

Ever wonder why we often justify our poor decisions? Psychology calls this cognitive dissonance. It's the mental discomfort experienced by a person who holds two or more contradictory beliefs, ideas, or values.

The Discomfort of Dissonance

Leon Festinger, the psychologist who pioneered the theory, argued that we have an inner drive to hold all our attitudes and beliefs in harmony and avoid disharmony (or dissonance). When we experience dissonance, we feel uncomfortable and are motivated to reduce it.

Reducing Dissonance

People reduce cognitive dissonance by:

  • Changing the conflicting belief.
  • Changing the conflicting behavior.
  • Adding new beliefs to justify the behavior.
For example, a smoker may justify their habit by saying "I only smoke a little" or "It's my only vice." This helps them feel better about their health.

Self-Justification

Understanding cognitive dissonance helps us recognize when we are making excuses for ourselves. By being aware of this mental trap, we can make more rational decisions and avoid self-deception.
